The Gum Story

An ancient harvest, a modern ingredient.

Gum Arabic, a resin from Acacia trees, has been treasured for centuries. Harvested mainly in Sudan, it acts as a natural binder and stabilizer — enriching foods, inks and medicines across the world.

This sustainable crop supports rural communities and bridges ancient tradition with modern industry. The harvest is timed to the dry season; producers tap acacia trees by hand and let the resin weep, harden and ripen under the African sun before collection.

The Gum Belt

Sudan dominates the world supply.

In Sudan, the Gum Belt covers Kordofan, Darfur, the White Nile and Blue Nile states — a sub-Saharan zone with ideal conditions for Acacia senegal (Hashab) and Acacia seyal (Talha). Sudan currently produces about 80% of the world's available Gum Arabic, and its grades are widely regarded as the finest in the global market.

Sudanese Heritage

A trade as old as the acacia.

Long before refined-sugar drinks or tablet binders existed, Sudanese tappers walked between acacia trees in the dry season — making careful incisions on the bark, returning weeks later to collect the amber-coloured tears the tree had wept and dried under the sun.

"The Gum Belt isn't just a place on a map. It's a living tradition — passed from father to son for generations."

— Kordofan, Sudan
